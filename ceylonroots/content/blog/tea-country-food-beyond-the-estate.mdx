At 1,900 metres above sea level, Nuwara Eliya mornings are cold in a way that surprises every visitor who arrives expecting Sri Lanka's coastal warmth. The cold is part of what shaped the food here. So is the history — both of it, in parallel: the Tamil plantation workers brought here from South India during British rule, and the British administrators who turned this highland valley into a replica of an English market town.

Both legacies persist in what you eat. The hill country rewards slow, curious eating more than anywhere else on the island.

## The Tamil Estate Kitchen

The Tamil communities who have worked the tea estates since the 19th century brought a cooking tradition rooted in South Indian techniques — but shaped over generations by what was available at altitude and what could be grown or foraged near the estates.

The **roti and curry** style in the hills is distinct from the Colombo or coastal versions. The rotis are thicker, slightly doughy, and cooked on stone griddles over wood fire. They are eaten with a thin, intensely spiced curry — often a simple dhal with fresh curry leaves, mustard seeds, and dried chilli — that has a directness and heat that refined restaurant kitchens tend to round off.

Small roadside stalls near estate entrances serve this combination for breakfast and lunch. Look for smoke, a griddle, and a woman who doesn't have a menu. That is always the right place.

The **string hoppers** at estate-adjacent tea shops — steam-pressed rice noodle nests — are served with a coconut milk curry that is thinner and lighter than the southern versions, better suited to the climate.

## British Colonial Leftovers

Nuwara Eliya was designed by British planters and administrators to feel like the English countryside transplanted to Asia. They brought seeds for strawberries, leeks, and carrots. They built a racecourse, a golf course, and hotels with fireplaces. They called it "Little England."

The food relics are extraordinary if you know where to look. The **Hill Club** — a colonial-era members' club that now accepts guests — serves a fixed-menu dinner in a dining room with mounted animal heads, dark wood panelling, and waiters in white jackets. The menu includes leek soup, bread rolls, and a roast meat option. It is colonial-era English cooking preserved in amber, and it is genuinely worth experiencing once for the sheer strangeness of eating Yorkshire pudding in the Sri Lankan highlands.

The **strawberry farms** around Nuwara Eliya sell fresh berries from roadside stalls with fresh cream, a habit that dates directly from British introduction. They are excellent in season (December–April).

## The Ella Café Scene: Navigating It

Ella has been discovered, and its food scene reflects that. The backpacker cafes serving smoothie bowls, banana roti with Nutella, and "Sri Lankan-inspired" fusion dishes have multiplied significantly. Much of it is fine but none of it is local.

The **banana roti** itself — roti with sliced banana, honey, and coconut cream — is genuinely a Sri Lankan invention and is worth eating at a place that does it properly. Avoid versions that arrive with a jar of peanut butter.

For local food in Ella, walk away from the main strip. The small curry shops on the road towards **Ella Rock trailhead** serve rice and curry to hikers and workers from early morning. The portions are large, the price is minimal, and the food is the real thing.

## What to Drink and How

The tea of the hill country is not the milky, sweet tea most international visitors associate with Sri Lanka. The correct approach is:

**Uva region teas** (grown at high elevation near Ella and Badulla) are known for an astringent, menthol-edged brightness — best drunk black, ideally without sugar on the first cup. Estate bungalows and factory shops sell single-origin teas that bear no resemblance to the export blends in tourist shops.

**Dimbula teas** (from the western hill slopes) are mellower and more floral. The **Nuwara Eliya teas** have a delicate, almost green-tea character at their finest. In season (January–March for Nuwara Eliya), the light, first-flush varieties are worth seeking out.

At small restaurants and tea shops, ask for **plain tea** (black tea without milk) or specify milk and sugar separately. The default "milk tea" served everywhere is good but hides the character of the leaves.

## Best Specific Stops

- **Hill Club dining room, Nuwara Eliya** — book ahead, go for the experience
- **Grand Indian Restaurant, Nuwara Eliya** — Tamil vegetarian food, excellent thali
- **Local dhal houses on the Hatton–Nuwara Eliya road** — no names, no signs, follow the steam
- **Ella Rock Café area stalls** — rice and curry from 7am, closes when the rice runs out

The hill country food scene rewards patience and wandering. Budget two full days minimum if eating is part of the point of being here.
