Most travelers to Sri Lanka's highlands make a beeline for Ella. The train, the Nine Arch Bridge, Little Adam's Peak — the Instagram circuit is well worn. The Knuckles Mountain Range sits just 30 kilometres from Kandy, and most people drive straight past it. That's exactly why it's worth stopping.

## What Makes the Knuckles Different

The Knuckles — named after the silhouette of a clenched fist visible from certain angles — forms part of a UNESCO World Heritage biosphere reserve. The range contains over 34 endemic bird species, rare purple-faced langur monkeys, and a microclimate that produces mist-shrouded cloud forests even in the dry season.

Unlike Ella, there are no smoothie cafes, no crowds at the viewpoints, and no queue for the train seats. The trails here are used by local farmers walking between villages, and occasionally by serious hikers who have done their research. You will almost certainly have them to yourself.

## Entry Points and Trail Options

**Rangala** is the most accessible entry point from Kandy — roughly 45 minutes by tuk-tuk. The trails from here are well-marked and moderate in difficulty, making it the best choice for first-time visitors or those without a guide.

**Riverston** sits at the northern edge of the range and is more suited to experienced hikers. The roads to get there are rougher, but the reward is the **Mini World's End** — a cliff-edge viewpoint that offers a sheer drop view rivalling the famous one in Horton Plains, without the bus tours.

**Deanston** offers longer multi-day routes for those who want to trek from village to village and stay with local families. This is the most immersive option but requires advance planning.

## Highlights Along the Trail

The **Corbetts Gap viewpoint** at dawn is one of the finest landscapes in Sri Lanka. You arrive in darkness, wait as the mist lifts, and then watch the peaks emerge in layers. Bring something warm — it will be cold.

The suspension bridges across the forest ravines are a recurring feature of Knuckles trails. They look dramatic but are solid. Cross slowly and the views down through the canopy are extraordinary.

Listen for the endemic **Knuckles pygmy lizard** on the forest floor, and check mossy logs for land snails found nowhere else in the world.

## Practical Logistics

Getting to the Knuckles requires either hiring a tuk-tuk from Kandy (negotiate a day rate) or joining a small-group guided trek from one of the eco-lodges in Rangala. There is no public bus that enters the reserve properly.

**Where to stay:** Small eco-lodges and community homestays in Rangala and Corbetts Gap are the right choice. They are run by local families, the food is home-cooked, and the proprietors double as guides. Avoid the larger resort hotels on the Kandy side — they add unnecessary distance and price.

**Permits:** A nominal entry permit is required at the reserve gate. Guides arranged through eco-lodges handle this automatically.

## When to Go

The driest and clearest months are **January through April**. The cloud forest remains atmospheric year-round, but heavy rain in June–August can make trails slippery and some routes impassable. If you visit during the wet season, invest in proper leech socks — they are sold in Kandy market and are non-negotiable.

Pack layers. Temperatures at the higher ridges drop sharply after dark and can feel surprisingly cold for a tropical country. Lightweight waterproofs, good footwear, and a willingness to start early are all you need. The Knuckles will do the rest.
